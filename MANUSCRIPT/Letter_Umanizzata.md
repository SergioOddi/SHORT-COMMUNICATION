To the Editor,

Preclinical-to-clinical attrition remains one of the most persistent failures in drug development — and sex bias in experimental design has become increasingly difficult to dismiss as a contributing factor [1,2]. A 2014 NIH mandate formally requiring sex as a biological variable [3] has changed remarkably little: male animals still dominate preclinical neuroscience and neuropharmacology [4]. Nowhere is this gap more consequential than in Alzheimer's disease. Women represent roughly two-thirds of AD patients and tend to follow a more aggressive disease trajectory than men [5] — yet the preclinical research underpinning current therapeutic strategies has been conducted almost exclusively in male animals. 

We recently showed that chronic Palmitoylethanolamide (PEA) treatment — delivered via subcutaneous slow-release pellets — confers broad neuroprotection in male Tg2576 mice, by attenuating neuroinflammation, reducing nitrosative stress, restoring hippocampal dendritic spine density, and rescuing cognitive deficits [6].

In this study, we applied the same protocol to female Tg2576 mice, concentrating on the three endpoints that had demonstrated the most pronounced response to PEA in males: (i) cognitive performance (NORT), (ii) hippocampal CA1 dendritic spine density, and (iii) 3-Nitro-L-tyrosine (3-NT) levels. Contrary to expectations, chronic administration of PEA did not replicate any of the beneficial effects observed in the behavioural, structural, and biochemical assessments. Instead, the data indicate an unforeseen, sex-dependent divergence in the neuroprotective response to PEA within this amyloidosis model.

In the NORT, PEA-treated Tg2576 females showed only a modest, non-significant improvement (59.1 ± 3.0% vs. 53.8 ± 1.9%; p = 0.496, d = 0.64) — roughly half the effect size we had observed in males (Fig.1A, [6]). Dendritic spine density was equally unresponsive: no significant rescue was detected for either apical (Tg Veh: 2.21 ± 0.49 vs. Tg PEA: 2.64 ± 0.92 n/μm; p = 0.37, d = 0.58) (Fig.1B) or basal (Tg Veh: 2.11 ± 0.34 vs. Tg PEA: 2.61 ± 0.80 n/μm; p = 0.12, d = 0.81) dendrites (Fig.1C), in stark contrast to the significant increase in basal dendritic spine density we had reported in hippocampal CA1 pyramidal neurons of male Tg2576 mice receiving the same treatment (Cliff's delta = 0.49, p = 0.008; Tortolani et al., 2025).
Finally, unlike what was previously reported in male Tg2576 mice — in which PEA produced a highly significant 30% reduction in hippocampal 3-NT levels (Cohen's d = 2.75, p = 0.0002; Tortolani et al., 2025) — the same chronic treatment failed to significantly reduce 3-NT expression in age-matched female Tg2576 mice (d = 0.70, p = 0.223).

This prompted a straightforward question: could sex differences in hippocampal PEA catabolism account for the blunted response in females? To address this, we quantified Fatty Acid Amide Hydrolase (FAAH) — the principal neuronal enzyme responsible for PEA hydrolysis — at the mRNA, protein, and enzymatic activity levels in male and female Tg2576 hippocampus.

The findings were consistent across all three readouts. Female Tg2576 mice showed approximately two-fold higher FAAH expression than males: mRNA (2.17×; p = 0.028) (Fig.1E), protein (2.15×; p = 0.0005) (Fig.1F-G), and enzymatic activity (2.12×; p = 0.005) (Fig.1H). Critically, this difference was not driven by the transgene expressed in Tg2576 mice — WT females also displayed significantly elevated FAAH protein relative to WT males (1.75×; p = 0.021; Supplementary Fig. S1), pointing to a constitutive, genotype-independent effect. The most reasonable interpretation is hormonal: estrogen response elements (ERE) recognized by estrogen receptors are present in the mouse Faah gene promoter (Supplementary Fig. S2) [7], consistent with sex hormone-driven regulation of PEA catabolism.

Although both FAAH and N-acylethanolamine acid amidase (NAAA) can hydrolyze PEA, their contributions are strongly tissue-dependent [8]. In the brain, FAAH — a membrane-bound serine hydrolase highly expressed in neurons — is the predominant catabolic enzyme. NAAA, by contrast, is a lysosomal cysteine hydrolase primarily expressed in immune cells, with optimal activity at acidic pH; its relevance increases mainly under conditions of immune cell infiltration [8,9]. Intracellular fatty acid binding proteins, moreover, do not shuttle exogenously administered PEA to lysosomal NAAA — making FAAH the primary degradation route for exogenous PEA in the central nervous system [10].

NAAA expression followed a parallel but attenuated pattern: a non-significant trend toward higher levels in females at both the mRNA (1.46×; p = 0.232) and protein (2.49×; p = 0.128) levels. The reduced magnitude of this sex difference may reflect a structural difference in promoter architecture — the NAAA promoter harbours only an ERα element, whereas the FAAH promoter contains both ERα and ERβ elements (Supplementary Fig. S2). This asymmetry further supports the interpretation that neuronal FAAH-mediated catabolism is the principal pharmacokinetic determinant of sex-dependent PEA efficacy in the brain.

These data offer a concrete, mechanistic illustration of why sex-stratified preclinical testing is a scientific necessity — not merely a matter of regulatory compliance. Evaluating neuroprotective compounds exclusively in male animals generates efficacy data that may not generalize to most AD patients, a systematic flaw with direct consequences for clinical translation [1]. In the case of PEA, the limiting factor is not target engagement or intrinsic efficacy. It is a druggable pharmacokinetic barrier: FAAH hyperactivity. This reframes the female non-response not as a therapeutic failure, but as a targetable mechanism of resistance — one that may be overcome through combination strategies incorporating FAAH inhibitors [8,9] or through ultramicronized PEA (UM-PEA) formulations with enhanced bioavailability [10].

Sex-dependent FAAH hyperactivity is a mechanistically defined reason why PEA neuroprotection attenuates in female Tg2576 mice. Beyond this specific compound, our findings argue for the routine inclusion of both sexes in preclinical AD studies and identify FAAH inhibition as a rational co-therapeutic strategy for female patients. More broadly, they reinforce the principle that pharmacological sex differences must be characterized at the preclinical stage — before insufficiently effective therapies reach the clinic.
