To the Editor,

Preclinical-to-clinical attrition remains one of the most persistent failures in drug development — and sex bias in experimental design has become increasingly difficult to dismiss as a contributing factor [1,2]. A 2014 NIH mandate formally requiring sex as a biological variable [3] has changed remarkably little: male animals still dominate preclinical neuroscience and neuropharmacology [4]. Nowhere is this gap more consequential than in Alzheimer's disease. Women represent roughly two-thirds of AD patients and tend to follow a more aggressive disease trajectory than men [5] — yet the preclinical research underpinning current therapeutic strategies has been conducted almost exclusively in male animals. 

We recently showed that chronic Palmitoylethanolamide (PEA) treatment — delivered via subcutaneous slow-release pellets — confers broad neuroprotection in male Tg2576 mice, by attenuating neuroinflammation, reducing nitrosative stress, restoring hippocampal dendritic spine density, and rescuing cognitive deficits [6].

In this study, we applied the same protocol to female Tg2576 mice, concentrating on the three endpoints that had demonstrated the most pronounced response to PEA in males: (i) cognitive performance (NORT), (ii) hippocampal CA1 dendritic spine density, and (iii) 3-Nitro-L-tyrosine (3-NT) levels. Contrary to expectations, chronic administration of PEA did not replicate any of the beneficial effects observed in the behavioural, structural, and biochemical assessments. Instead, the data indicate an unforeseen, sex-dependent divergence in the neuroprotective response to PEA within this amyloidosis model.

Inaspettatamente, chronic PEA-treated Tg2576 females showed a modest, non-significant improvement (59.1 ± 3.0% vs. 53.8 ± 1.9%; p = 0.496, d = 0.64), yielding an effect size approximately half that we had observed in Tg2576 males (Fig.1A, [6]). Allo stesso modo, we did not observe any significant rescue in Tg2576 females for either apical (Tg Veh: 2.21 ± 0.49 vs. Tg PEA: 2.64 ± 0.92 n/μm; p = 0.37, d = 0.58) (Fig.1B) or basal (Tg Veh: 2.11 ± 0.34 vs. Tg PEA: 2.61 ± 0.80 n/μm; p = 0.12, d = 0.81) dendrites (Fig.1C), contrariamente a quanto avevamo riscontrato nei maschi in cui lo stesso trattamento increased basal dendritic spine density in hippocampal CA1 pyramidal neurons (Cliff's delta = 0.49, p = 0.008; Tortolani et al., 2025).
Finally, unlike what was previously reported in male Tg2576 mice — in which PEA produced a highly significant 30% reduction in hippocampal 3-NT levels (Cohen's d = 2.75, p = 0.0002; Tortolani et al., 2025) — the same chronic treatment failed to significantly reduce 3-NT expression in age-matched female Tg2576 mice (d = 0.70, p = 0.223).
