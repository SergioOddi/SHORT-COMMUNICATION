To the Editor,
----
Most preclinical drug candidates fail during clinical translation, and sex bias in preclinical research has been identified as a key contributing factor [1, 2]. Despite the US National Institutes of Health (NIH) mandating consideration of sex as a biological variable in 2014, most preclinical neuroscience and neuropharmacology studies still rely exclusively or predominantly on male subjects [4]. This is particularly significant in the case of Alzheimer's disease (AD), where women account for around two-thirds of patients and tend to experience more severe cognitive decline than men [5]. However, most therapeutic strategies are developed and validated using male animals, creating a critical gap that hinders the development of effective treatments for female patients.

We recently demonstrated that chronic palmitoylethanolamide (PEA) administration via subcutaneous slow-release pellets exerts broad neuroprotective effects in male Tg2576 mice. These effects include attenuation of neuroinflammation, reduction of nitrosative stress, restoration of hippocampal dendritic spine density and rescue of cognitive deficits [6]. Here, we applied the same protocol to female Tg2576 mice and assessed the core endpoints of cognitive performance (NORT), CA1 dendritic spine density and 3-nitrotyrosine (3-NT) levels, which had previously been shown to be most responsive to PEA treatment in males. However, contrary to our previous findings, chronic PEA administration failed to reproduce the same beneficial effects across these behavioural, structural and biochemical readouts. This revealed a striking sex-dependent divergence in the neuroprotective response to PEA in this amyloidosis model.

PEA treatment produced markedly different cognitive outcomes depending on sex. In male Tg2576 mice, PEA significantly improved discrimination index in the NORT compared to vehicle (p = 0.017, Cohen’s d = 1.39) [6]. In contrast, PEA-treated females showed a modest, non-significant improvement (59.1 ± 3.0% vs. 53.8 ± 1.9%; p = 0.496, d = 0.64), yielding an effect size approximately half that observed in males (Fig.1A).

While chronic PEA treatment restored apical (p = 0.012) and basal (p = 0.026) dendritic spine density in male Tg2576 mice [6], no significant rescue was observed in females for either apical (Tg Veh: 2.21 ± 0.49 vs. Tg PEA: 2.64 ± 0.92 n/μm; p = 0.37, d = 0.58) (Fig.1B) or basal (Tg Veh: 2.11 ± 0.34 vs. Tg PEA: 2.61 ± 0.80 n/μm; p = 0.12, d = 0.81) dendrites (Fig.1C), consistent with the attenuated cognitive response.
PEA reduced hippocampal 3-NT levels in both sexes in proportional terms (−30.4% in males, −20.6% in females), but this reduction reached statistical significance only in males (13.3 ± 0.8 vs. 19.1 ± 0.8; p = 0.0002, d = −2.97) and not in females (7.7 ± 0.8 vs. 9.7 ± 1.3; p = 0.218, d = −0.75) (Fig.1D). Since nitrosative stress has been proposed as an upstream driver of synaptic dysfunction in AD, potentially through calcineurin-dependent mechanisms [6], the attenuated reduction of 3-NT in females may contribute to the lack of significant spine rescue observed above, suggesting a link between diminished antioxidant efficacy and impaired structural synaptic recovery.

The results revealed a substantially attenuated therapeutic response in females across multiple endpoints. This unexpected finding prompted us to investigate whether sex differences in hippocampal PEA catabolism could account for the reduced efficacy, leading us to quantify Fatty Acid Amide Hydrolase (FAAH), the principal neuronal enzyme responsible for PEA hydrolysis at the mRNA, protein, and enzymatic activity levels in male versus female hippocampus of Tg2576 mice.

Female Tg2576 mice exhibited approximately two-fold higher FAAH mRNA (2.17×; p = 0.028) (Fig.1E), protein levels (2.15×; p = 0.0005) (Fig.1F-G), and enzymatic activity (2.12×; p = 0.005) compared to males (Fig.1H). This sex difference was independent of the Tg2576 genotype, as wild-type (WT) females also displayed significantly elevated FAAH protein levels relative to WT males (1.75×; p = 0.021; Supplementary Fig. S1). This is consistent with a constitutive, hormonally driven phenomenon, supported by the presence of both α-adrenergic and β-adrenergic receptors (ERα and Erβ) responsive elements in the mouse FAAH gene promoter (Supplementary Fig. S2) [7].

Importantly, although both FAAH and N-acylethanolamine acid amidase (NAAA) can hydrolyze PEA, their contributions are strongly tissue-dependent [8]. In the brain, FAAH a membrane-bound serine hydrolase highly expressed in neurons, is the predominant catabolic enzyme, whereas NAAA is a lysosomal cysteine hydrolase primarily expressed in immune cells with optimal activity at acidic pH, gaining relevance mainly under conditions of immune cell infiltration [8,9]. Moreover, intracellular fatty acid binding proteins do not deliver exogenously administered PEA to lysosomal NAAA, establishing FAAH as the primary degradation route for exogenous PEA in the central nervous system [10]. 

Consistent with this, NAAA expression showed a non-significant trend toward higher levels in females at both the mRNA (1.46×; p = 0.232) and protein (2.49×; p = 0.128) level; a weaker sex difference that may reflect the presence of only an ERα element in the NAAA promoter, compared to both ERα and ERβ elements in the FAAH promoter (Supplementary Fig. S2) reinforcing the interpretation that neuronal FAAH-mediated catabolism is the principal pharmacokinetic determinant of sex-dependent PEA efficacy in the brain.
Our findings provide a concrete, mechanistic illustration of why sex-stratified preclinical testing is not merely a matter of policy compliance but a scientific necessity. The prevailing practice of evaluating neuroprotective compounds exclusively in male animals generates efficacy data that may not generalize to most AD patients, a systematic flaw that contributes to the well-documented crisis of clinical translation [1]. Our data demonstrate that a compound showing robust neuroprotection in males can be substantially less effective in females due to a specific, druggable pharmacokinetic barrier: FAAH hyperactivity. This is not a blanket therapeutic failure but rather a targetable mechanism of resistance, suggesting that female AD patients may benefit from combination therapies incorporating FAAH inhibitors [8,9] or from ultramicronized PEA (UM-PEA) formulations with enhanced bioavailability [10]. 

In summary, sex-dependent FAAH hyperactivity substantially attenuates PEA neuroprotection in female Tg2576 mice. Our findings mandate the routine inclusion of both sexes in preclinical AD studies, identify FAAH inhibition as a rational co-therapeutic strategy for female patients, and underscore the broader principle that pharmacological sex differences must be addressed at the preclinical stage to avoid propagating ineffective therapies into clinical trials.
