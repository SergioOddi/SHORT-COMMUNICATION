To the Editor,

Most preclinical drug candidates fail during clinical translation, with sex bias in preclinical research identified as a key contributing factor [1,2]. Indeed, although the US National Institutes of Health (NIH) mandated consideration of sex as a biological variable in 2014 [3], most preclinical studies in neuroscience and neuropharmacology still rely exclusively, or mainly, on male subjects [4]. This imbalance is especially significant in Alzheimer's disease (AD), where women represent about two-thirds of patients and often experience more severe cognitive decline than men [5]. However, most therapeutic strategies are developed and validated using male animals, creating a critical gap that hinders effective treatment for female patients.

We recently demonstrated that chronic Palmitoylethanolamide (PEA) administration via subcutaneous slow-release pellets exerts broad neuroprotective effects in male Tg2576 mice, including attenuation of neuroinflammation, reduction of nitrosative stress, restoration of hippocampal dendritic spine density, and rescue of cognitive deficits [6].

We recently demonstrated that chronic Palmitoylethanolamide (PEA) administration via subcutaneous slow-release pellets exerts broad neuroprotective effects in male Tg2576 mice, including attenuation of neuroinflammation, reduction of nitrosative stress, restoration of hippocampal dendritic spine density, and rescue of cognitive deficits [6]. Here, we applied the identical protocol to female Tg2576 mice and assessed the same core endpoints — cognitive performance (NORT), hippocampal CA1 dendritic spine density, and 3-Nitro-L-tyrosine (3-NT) level — that had proven most responsive to PEA treatment in males. Contrary to our previous findings, however, chronic PEA administration failed to reproduce the same beneficial effects across these behavioral, structural, and biochemical readouts, revealing a striking sex-dependent divergence in the neuroprotective response to PEA in this amyloidosis model.
